## The situation

You are reviewing how a company directs its IT. There is an IT steering committee, a strategy document, and a CIO who clearly runs a tight operation. Everything looks healthy. Then you notice the committee reports to the CIO, and the board has not discussed IT in over a year.

Nothing is technically broken. But the governance is, and the exam wants you to see why.

## Governance is not management

This is the distinction the whole domain rests on, and most Domain 2 questions turn on it.

**Governance** sets direction and holds people accountable for following it. It is the board's job. Governance answers: what should IT achieve for the enterprise, what risk are we willing to carry, and is management actually delivering it?

**Management** runs IT within that direction. It is the CIO's job. Management answers: how do we deliver what governance asked for, with the resources we have?

The exam draws this line constantly. When an option has the board designing controls, selecting technologies, or monitoring service levels, it is wrong, because those are management activities. When an option has management setting the enterprise's risk appetite or accepting strategic risk on the organisation's behalf, that is wrong too, because those belong to governance.

## What the board cannot delegate

A board can delegate authority to an IT strategy committee or a steering committee. What it cannot delegate is **accountability**. The board remains answerable to shareholders and regulators for the value IT delivers and the risk it carries, no matter how much decision-making it hands down.

The board's own responsibilities, the ones that stay at the top, are to:

- **Set the strategic direction** for IT so it supports the enterprise's objectives.
- **Ensure IT delivers value** and that investment is justified against business benefit.
- **Ensure IT risk is managed** within an appetite the board has defined.
- **Hold management accountable** through reporting it can actually rely on.

## Reporting lines betray governance

The fastest way to spot broken governance is to follow the reporting line, exactly as you would for the audit function in Domain 1.

An IT steering committee exists to align IT spending and priorities with business needs, on behalf of the enterprise. If that committee is chaired by the CIO and reports to the CIO, it oversees IT while answering to the head of IT. It cannot provide the independent, business-driven direction that is its entire purpose. The structure has quietly turned oversight into self-endorsement.

The same logic flags a board that has not considered IT in a year: direction that is never revisited is not governance, it is a document.

## Where practitioners go wrong

The instinct that costs marks is judging governance by how well IT is run. A superbly managed IT function with no board oversight is still a governance failure, because good management is not a substitute for accountability. The exam rewards separating the two: ask not "is IT working well" but "who set the direction, and who holds them to it".

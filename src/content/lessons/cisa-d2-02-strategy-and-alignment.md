## The situation

The IT strategy document is genuinely impressive: cloud migration, a data platform, an AI roadmap. The technology choices are current and the ambition is real. You are inclined to conclude the strategy is strong.

Then you ask a simple question of each initiative, what business objective does this serve, and the answers get vague. That vagueness is the finding.

## Alignment is traceability

Business-IT alignment sounds abstract until you make it concrete: **every significant IT initiative should trace back to a business objective it advances.** IT does not exist to be modern; it exists to help the enterprise meet its goals.

So the auditor's test for alignment is not "is this strategy ambitious" but "can each initiative be tied to a business need". A cloud migration that reduces the cost of a service the business is trying to scale is aligned. The same migration pursued because the platform is end-of-life, with no business case, is not aligned, it is maintenance wearing a strategy's clothes.

This is why the exam rewards traceability over sophistication. A plain strategy where every line serves a stated business goal beats a dazzling one that serves the technology.

## Who sets strategy, and who steers it

Two committees appear constantly in Domain 2, and the exam expects you to tell them apart.

**The IT strategy committee** operates at **board level**. It advises the board on the future direction of IT and on whether IT strategy supports business strategy. It is concerned with direction.

**The IT steering committee** operates **below the board**. It turns that direction into prioritised, resourced execution: which initiatives proceed, in what order, with what budget. It is concerned with delivery.

The shortcut: strategy committee advises the board on direction; steering committee prioritises and oversees the execution. Confusing the two is a reliable exam trap, because both are legitimate and both concern IT investment.

## Technology-driven is the failure mode

The clearest sign of misalignment is the order in which decisions get made. When the IT department selects projects on technical merit and consults business units only after approval, selection is **technology-driven**. The business need never shaped what was chosen, so the most significant risk is that the investment does not deliver business value, however well built it is.

Business-driven selection reverses that order: the business states a need, and IT proposes how to meet it. Alignment is as much about sequence as about content.

## Where practitioners go wrong

The instinct that costs marks is admiring the strategy on its own terms. An auditor impressed by the roadmap asks whether the technology is good. The exam asks whether the technology serves the business. Judge an IT strategy by its traceability to business objectives, not by how advanced or ambitious it looks.
